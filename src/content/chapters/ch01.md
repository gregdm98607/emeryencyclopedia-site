**1.1 County Boundaries & Coordinate Reference Frame**
Emery County encompasses approximately 4,472 square miles (11,580 km²) in east-central Utah. Its legal boundary is defined by Utah Code § 17‑16‑6, specifying PLSS lines tied to the Salt Lake Meridian and Base Line. The northwest corner is located at Township 12 South, Range 5 East; the southeast corner at Township 28 South, Range 19 East. Spatial data use NAD 83 (2011) for horizontal control and NAVD 88 for elevations. This framework supports all cadastral, mapping, and geospatial analyses in the county.

**1.2 Physiographic Provinces**
Emery County lies at the intersection of three major land‐form provinces: the Basin‑and‑Range Great Basin to the west, the Colorado Plateau to the east, and the northern margin of the Middle Rocky Mountains along the Wasatch Plateau crest. Basin‐range topography is evident in internally drained Castle and Gunnison Valleys, while sedimentary strata of the Colorado Plateau appear in the Book Cliffs and San Rafael Swell. Elevation rises from about 4,100 ft to over 11,300 ft within 60 miles, illustrating the county's dynamic tectonic mosaic.

**1.3 Major Landforms & Relief**
Three principal features define Emery County's relief: the forested Wasatch Plateau, the sandstone escarpment of the Book Cliffs/Tavaputs Plateau, and the monoclinal fold of the San Rafael Swell. Elevation increases from 4,100 ft (1,250 m) along the Green River floodplain to 11,300 ft (3,444 m) on the Wasatch–La Sal divide. The Swell's forelimb exceeds 2,000 ft of relief, and the Book Cliffs present a near‐vertical face extending over 60 miles. These structures reflect Laramide uplift, Basin‑and‑Range extension, and differential erosion, which together control drainage alignment, soil development, and vegetation patterns.

**1.4 Drainage Basins & Watersheds**
Three primary river systems—the San Rafael, Muddy, and Price rivers—drain Emery County into the Green River, supplemented by numerous ephemeral washes. The San Rafael River originates on the Manti–La Sal divide, incises Gray Canyon through Navajo Sandstone, and sustains riparian corridors of Fremont cottonwood (Populus fremontii) and narrowleaf willow (Salix exigua). Muddy Creek begins near Scofield Reservoir and carves through Entrada formations before depositing sediment pulses in Emory Canyon. The Price River's tributaries (Ferron, Cottonwood, Huntington creeks) are regulated by reservoirs and diversions supporting irrigation in Ferron and Castle Dale. Ephemeral drainages such as Little Wild Horse and Buckhorn Wash sculpt alluvial fans and contribute to canyon development. Mapping watersheds as USGS HUC‑12 units and analyzing seasonal flow records are essential for water‐resource management, habitat restoration, and flood hazard mitigation.

**1.5 Elevation Gradient & Hypsometry**
Emery County's elevation ranges from ~4,100 ft on the Green River floodplain to 11,300 ft on the Wasatch–La Sal divide, a 7,200‑ft span over 60 miles. Hypsometric analysis shows ~30% of area below 5,000 ft, 50% between 5,000 and 8,000 ft, and 20% above 8,000 ft. This distribution governs climate, vegetation, and snowpack dynamics. South‐facing slopes average 2–3 °C warmer than equivalent north‐facing aspects, influencing species zonation. These elevation‐aspect patterns inform land management, ecological modeling, and recreational planning.**1.6 Structural Geology: Uplifts, Folds & Faults**
Emery County's structural fabric records Laramide compression and subsequent Basin‑and‑Range extension. The San Rafael Swell is a 75‑mile‐long monoclinal anticline uplifting Paleozoic strata. The Book Cliffs escarpment marks the Tavaputs Plateau limb, bounded by Late Cretaceous basement‐involved thrusts. The Wasatch Plateau block is defined by east‐dipping normal faults from extensional tectonics. Secondary faults like the Cleveland and Clawson offsets displace Quaternary alluvium. The Ferron Thrust juxtaposes Mancos Shale over coal‐bearing Ferron Sandstone, influencing resource distribution. Modern geophysical surveys and LiDAR reveal detailed geometry and guide resource management.

**1.7 Geomorphology of the San Rafael Swell**
The San Rafael Swell is a 75 × 40‑mile monoclinal arch formed during the Laramide Orogeny (70–60 Ma). Resistant Navajo and Entrada sandstones form hogbacks and domes, while softer Mancos and Carmel shales underlie benches and talus. Fluvial incision by the San Rafael River and tributaries creates narrow V‐shaped canyons, controlled by joint patterns. Karst features in limestone units produce sinkholes and small caves with speleothems. Aeolian processes carve arches and domes in upper Navajo sandstone. Uplift estimates (1,500–2,000 ft) derive from cross‐section restoration. High‐resolution DEMs enhance hazard mapping and interpretive trail design.

**1.8 Soil Orders & Geochemistry**
Aridisols dominate low basins, with thin A horizons over carbonate‐rich subsoils (Torriorthents, Torrifluvents). Pinyon‑juniper slopes host Entisols and Inceptisols with mixed grain sizes and iron‐oxide coatings. Higher elevations support Alfisols and Mollisols under conifer forests and meadows. Soil pH ranges from ~7.8 in basins to ~5.5 in spruce‐fir zones; organic content increases upslope. Geochemical analyses reveal elevated sodium adsorption ratios on fans and carbonate saturation in valley soils, informing agriculture and infrastructure planning.

**1.9 Karst & Cave Systems**
Karst development occurs where Mississippian and Pennsylvanian limestones outcrop along the Wasatch Plateau margin. Sinkholes, solution pits, and solution‐enhanced caves (<100 ft passages) form in Leadville and Weber formations. Dye‐trace studies connect ridge recharge to springs, highlighting groundwater flow paths. Karst springs feed riparian habitats and host specialized fauna. Archaeological deposits in rock shelters record Fremont‐period use. LIDAR and field surveys map karst features critical for water resource management and heritage conservation.

**1.10 Aeolian Landforms & Desert Pavements**
Wind shapes Emery County's deserts through dunes, pavements, and loess. Active and stabilized dunes occur in Roberts Roost Flats and Little Wild Horse, aligned with spring winds. Desert pavements cap benches near Ferron and Huntington, preserving surface fabrics and varnish. Loess deposits (<30 cm) on lee slopes near Castle Dale support unique vegetation. Aerial imagery and DEM analysis inform restoration and OHV planning by mapping sediment sources and transport pathways.

**1.11 Paleoflood Terraces & Alluvial Fans**
Terraces up to 15 ft above modern channels record Holocene floods along the Price and San Rafael rivers. Terrace treads consist of cobble–sand sequences overlain by overbank silts, dated by radiocarbon charcoal. Alluvial fans at Buckhorn Draw and Little Wild Horse show classic cone geometry, with proximal boulder lobes and distal sand aprons. Fan morphology reflects flash‐flood frequency and lithology, guiding flood risk mapping and infrastructure design.

**1.12 Geospatial Datasets & Mapping Standards**
All spatial data use NAD 83 and NAVD 88, primarily projected in UTM Zone 12N. Key vector layers include administrative boundaries, PLSS parcels, hydrology (NHD), and transportation. Raster products feature 10 m DEMs, 0.5–2 m LiDAR, and 1 m NAIP orthophotos. Soils data (SSURGO, STATSGO) and cultural resource shapefiles integrate with standard layers. Metadata follow FGDC CSDGM and ISO 19115. Web services (WMS/WFS) and tile caches support field GIS. Geodatabases use file geodatabase format with topology and versioning for multiuser editing.
